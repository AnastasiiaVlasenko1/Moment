# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

The primary user is an individual maker — a designer, engineer, or PM — logging their own work moments as the week goes. Their context is the middle of real work: something interesting comes up, a challenge lands, a small win happens, and they want to note it in a few seconds without leaving the headspace they're in. The seed projects (Mobile App, Design System, Marketing Site) reflect this: a person on a product team, working across a handful of ongoing efforts.

This is a single-person tool. There is no team view, no shared feed, no audience beyond the person writing — the moments belong to whoever captured them.

## Product Purpose

Moments is a personal work-journal built around a weekly rhythm of capture and a monthly rhythm of reflection. During the week, the Capture view lets someone log short, categorized moments — interesting, challenge, achievement, learning, mood — against the projects they're working on. At month's end, the Review view assembles those moments into a coherent look back: per-project highlights, what was learned, a mood arc, and an optional AI-written prose summary.

Success is a sustained retro habit. The user reaches the end of a week or month and, instead of straining to remember what they did, reads back a rich, honest account of how the time actually went — what went well, what was hard, what they learned. The review being worth reading is the measure; the capture existing only to feed it.

## Positioning

A work journal that feels warm and personal, not corporate. Every screen should read as a diary you want to open rather than a tracking tool you have to update. The differentiation is emotional posture as much as function: reflection over metrics, a private page over a dashboard. Capture stays effortless so the month-end review can be rich — the review is the payoff, and the whole product is arranged to earn it.

## Brand Personality

Warm, calm, personal. The voice is that of a private diary — unhurried and gentle, speaking to one person about their own work, never performing for an audience or managing them. Moments should evoke the quiet satisfaction of writing something down and, later, the small warmth of reading it back. It celebrates reflection over productivity theater: no urgency, no pressure, no scoreboard. Where a typical work tool is brisk and transactional, Moments is patient and human.

## Anti-references

Moments should explicitly not look or feel like:

- **Corporate SaaS dashboards** — cold blue-gray enterprise tooling, KPI walls, Jira/analytics-style density. This is the register the warm-diary intent exists to reject.
- **The generic AI-tool aesthetic** — dark mode with purple gradients, neon accents, glassmorphism, and gradient text. The AI prose summary is a feature, but the product must never wear the 2025 AI-startup costume.
- **A social feed or anything performative** — likes, public sharing, comparison, follower-style feeds. Moments is private and personal; the moments are written for the person who lived them, not for an audience.

## Design Principles

Capture must be nearly frictionless, because the month-end review is the payoff. Any weight added to logging a moment is weight subtracted from the habit, and the habit is what makes the review worth reading. Optimize the capture path relentlessly for speed and low commitment.

Warm over corporate, on every screen. When a choice is between the efficient-but-cold option and the personal-but-warm one, the product leans warm — this is the positioning made concrete, not a decoration to be dropped under deadline.

Reflection over metrics. Surface meaning, narrative, and pattern rather than scores, streaks, or productivity theater. Do not gamify the habit; let the value be the looking-back itself.

Private by default. Design for an audience of one. No feature should imply an outside reader, and nothing should invite comparison or performance.

Calm and legible. Nothing shouts. Restraint in color, motion, and density keeps the surface quiet enough that reflection can come forward — which is also what keeps it accessible.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.2 AA. Body text meets ≥4.5:1 contrast (large text ≥3:1), focus states are always visible, and the app is fully keyboard-navigable. Honor prefers-reduced-motion with a crossfade or instant alternative for every animation. The five categorical accents carry meaning, so category is always paired with an icon and label — never conveyed by color alone — so the distinctions survive color-blindness.
